Thinking in Resources

A Resource can be any real-world object or concept, or a piece of information about a real-world object.

Resources form nested hierarchies, or webs.

Identifiers

A resource can have many identifiers, but the Uniform Resource Identifier (URI) is by far the most common. Another common type of identifier is the URN, which is also a type of URI.

                            
URI: https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0076759/
URI: http://www.omdbapi.com/?i=tt0076759
URN: urn:isan:0000-0000-EB93-0000-Q-0000-0000-X

For most practical purposes HTTP URIs can also be thought of as the address of, or path to, the resource. There is, however, no way to tell if two different URIs refer to the same thing.

Representations

We're mostly concerned with information resources, which contain a view of the state of a real-world thing at a point in time.

Semantics

Document formats matter!

The meaning behind data and information must be shared between the producer and the consumer of a message.

Syntax vs. Semantics

Syntactically correct, but meaningless


                    {"blart": 3.92, "blech": "bloop"}

Semantically clear


                        {"cost": 3.92, "name": "bloop"}

Data formats

Producers and consumers need to agree on how data is structured and formatted, as well as its meaning.

Sidebar: Data, information and Knowledge

Data
Raw, uninterpreted "facts" e.g. an invoice, or customer record.
Information
The interpretation of data in a business context, like when courseDetails appears on an application, it's understood to be that application's course data.
